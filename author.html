Abstract. Microtransit routing platforms typically consume OpenStreetMap (OSM) as a base-map layer. In Hamilton County, Ohio, the Southwest Ohio Regional Transit Authority (SORTA) operates the MetroNow microtransit service in partnership with Via Transportation; the latter's routing engine ingests OSM data including a substantial population of road segments seeded by the 2007–2008 U.S. Census Bureau TIGER/Line bulk import and never subsequently verified by a human editor. This work performs a read-only audit of every such segment inside the four MetroNow service-zone bounding boxes via the Overpass API, classifies each segment against a four-class taxonomy (A false-one-way, B multi-segment, AB compound, C unreviewed-without-signal), and runs an endpoint-distance heuristic to flag probable node disconnects between same-named segments. Across the four zones, 6,096 unique unreviewed ways are identified (7,213 before zone-overlap deduplication), of which 221 are compound (Class AB) defects and 710 are unique probable node disconnects. The author's own residential address contains a documented compound defect that produces a reproducible MetroNow routing failure. The audit is presented as material for the OpenStreetMap volunteer community to evaluate and remediate; the pipeline does not mutate OSM and the heuristics are not assertions of ground truth.

1. Introduction

Public-transit microtransit services increasingly depend on routing engines whose base maps are sourced from OpenStreetMap (OSM)1. The data quality of those base maps therefore directly affects whether transit-dependent riders receive routes that successfully terminate at their addresses. This work is motivated by a single, reproducible failure mode: when a routing engine encounters a residential road segment carrying a false oneway=yes tag, it computes ingress with no legal egress and may declare the address unreachable. For a transit-dependent rider, the operational distinction between “unreachable” and “denied service” is moot.

The author observed this failure mode at his own residential address in Deer Park, OH—a community within the Blue Ash / Montgomery MetroNow service zone—and traced it to a residential way carrying both a false oneway=yes tag and an unjoined intersection node, both inherited from the 2007–2008 TIGER/Line Census import and never subsequently verified by an OSM editor. The investigation broadened from a single street to a four-zone audit. The contribution of this work is the methodology and the resulting deduplicated, machine-readable defect inventory.

2. Background

2.1 TIGER/Line and the 2007–2008 OSM bulk import

The U.S. Census Bureau's TIGER/Line dataset is a public-domain national road graph, originally constructed for the 1990 decennial census and updated periodically thereafter. Between 2007 and 2008 a near-complete TIGER/Line snapshot was bulk-imported into OSM2. Each imported way received the tag tiger:reviewed=no, indicating that no human editor had subsequently verified the geometry, connectivity, or attribute set against ground truth. Nearly two decades on, a non-trivial fraction of those ways persist in this unreviewed state, particularly in residential areas with low contributor density.

Two failure modes recur in the unreviewed population3:

  1. Erroneous one-way restrictions, particularly on dead-end residential streets and cul-de-sacs that the import classified as one-way without ground-truth verification.
  2. Disconnected nodes, in which two ways representing segments of the same physical street terminate within a few metres of each other but are not joined by a shared OSM node, leaving the routing graph topologically disconnected.

2.2 SORTA MetroNow and the Via routing platform

SORTA's MetroNow microtransit service is funded by Hamilton County's Issue 7 (2020) levy—a 0.8 % sales tax approved by referendum—and operated under contract by Via Transportation, Inc. Via's routing engine consumes OSM as its base map. Via additionally markets a proprietary layer (“ViaMapping”) that augments OSM with speed data, turn restrictions, and points of interest; based on publicly available ViaMapping documentation, the layer is not described as performing a road-graph topology audit, verifying one-way tags, or detecting disconnected nodes4. The contractual division of road-graph QA responsibility between SORTA and Via is not publicly visible at the time of this writing.

2.3 Why the audit is heuristic

An OSM defect cannot be confirmed in the abstract; the canonical referent is the road as it actually exists. This audit therefore produces candidates for review, not assertions of error. Each candidate is supported by deep links to the iD editor and to JOSM Remote Control, so that a human editor can evaluate the defect against satellite imagery, street-level photography (Mapillary, KartaView), and personal field knowledge before committing any change to OSM.

3. Methodology

3.1 Data acquisition

For each MetroNow service zone Z with bounding box (s, w, n, e), a single Overpass API query is issued:

[out:json][timeout:180];
way["highway"]["tiger:reviewed"="no"]
  (s, w, n, e);
out geom tags;

The out geom tags directive returns the full node coordinate array for each way inline, enabling endpoint-based topology analysis without a second round-trip. The primary endpoint is https://overpass-api.de/api/interpreter; on failure the pipeline retries against the kumi.systems mirror and, only on full upstream failure, falls back to the most recent locally cached snapshot (with a console warning if the cache exceeds 14 days of age). JSON-null responses are treated as retryable rather than as successful empty fetches. Each successful fetch is persisted as a timestamped JSON snapshot for reproducibility; snapshots older than 14 days are auto-pruned with the three most recent retained per zone.

3.2 Defect classification

Each unreviewed way w is assigned to exactly one of four disjoint classes:

3.3 Probable-node-disconnect detection

Within each Class B street (a maximal set of ways sharing a normalised name), the pipeline enumerates unordered pairs of ways and computes the minimum great-circle distance between the four candidate endpoint pairings using the haversine formula:

d = 2R · arcsin(√(sin²(Δφ/2) + cosφ₁ · cosφ₂ · sin²(Δλ/2)))

where R = 6,371,000 m is the WGS-84 mean Earth radius, φ denotes latitude in radians, and λ denotes longitude in radians. Pairs whose minimum endpoint distance lies in the half-open interval (0.01 m, 30 m] are flagged as probable disconnects. The lower bound excludes pairs that are already coincident; the upper bound is a heuristic chosen to capture near-misses while excluding endpoints that are merely on different parts of a long road.

A naive pairwise emission produces k(k−1)/2 records for a junction at which k ≥ 3 ways meet without a shared node. Because the audit is intended to surface physical disconnects rather than way-pair candidates, a clustering pass is applied: per-street pairwise records are sorted ascending by gap distance and any record whose midpoint lies within 5 m of an already-emitted cluster representative is discarded. The retained representative is therefore the tightest pair at each physical junction. The clustering threshold (5 m) was selected to be wider than residential lane-width but narrower than typical adjacent-junction spacing.

3.4 Cross-zone deduplication

The four MetroNow zone bounding boxes overlap in at least three pairwise regions, of which the Northgate / Mt. Healthy ↔ Forest Park / Pleasant Run overlap (~32 km²) and the Springdale / Sharonville ↔ Forest Park / Pleasant Run overlap (~14.5 km²) are non-trivial. A way that lies inside the union of two zone bboxes appears in both per-zone audits. Per-zone counts are correct as zone-specific totals; system-wide totals must therefore be derived from a deduplicated population.

Three independent dedup operations are applied for the combined-zone view:

  1. Way deduplication by OSM way ID. The merged inventory contains each unique way exactly once.
  2. Multi-segment-street counting by connected-components analysis over the (street name, way-ID set) groups, transitively coalescing components that share at least one way ID. An arterial that crosses a zone boundary collapses to a single street; two unrelated streets that happen to share a name in zones with disjoint way-ID populations are counted separately.
  3. Gap deduplication in two passes: first by unordered (wayi, wayj) pair, then by 5 m spatial proximity per street. The second pass catches a boundary edge case in which two zones independently elect different “tightest pair” representatives for the same physical junction.

4.2 Distribution of unreviewed ways

Across the four zones, a majority of unreviewed ways are residential (3,583 / 6,096 = 58.8 %). The compound-defect (Class AB) population is small in absolute terms (221 ways across 522 multi-segment streets, of which 20 streets in Blue Ash / Montgomery alone carry the original index case) but is precisely the population most likely to produce complete routing failures, because each compound-defect way carries both a false legal-direction restriction and an adjacent topology gap. Class B and Class A-only populations are larger but each individual defect produces a less catastrophic failure mode (a misrouted approach rather than a complete unreachability).

4.3 The Blue Ash / Montgomery index case

The author's residential street—O'Leary Avenue, in Deer Park—is one of the 2 single-segment Class A ways flagged in the Blue Ash / Montgomery zone. The street carries oneway=yes in OSM despite being two-way on the ground, and a near-miss endpoint at the intersection with the principal cross street is below the 30 m gap threshold but above the coincidence floor. The combination of these two defects is sufficient to produce a routing failure for any MetroNow journey terminating at the address, which the author has verified by direct observation of the dispatched vehicle's path.

5. Discussion

5.1 Limitations

The audit's heuristics are deliberately conservative; the population of true defects is almost certainly broader than the population this audit flags. In particular:

  • Heuristic, not ground-truth. The presence of tiger:reviewed=no indicates the absence of human verification; it does not entail the presence of an error. A way carrying the tag may nevertheless be entirely correct. Conversely, a way that has lost the tag (because some prior editor removed it without verifying the geometry) is invisible to this audit and may still contain defects.
  • Class B grouping by name string. The grouping of ways into multi-segment streets relies on case-insensitive equality of the name tag. Streets that share a name across non-adjacent geographies (e.g. a generic name appearing in two unrelated zones) are correctly separated by the connected-components dedup at the system level but may be merged incorrectly within a single zone if the zone bbox happens to include disjoint segments of two same-named streets.
  • Endpoint-only gap detection. The 30 m threshold catches only gaps at way endpoints; mid-way disconnects (e.g. a short cross-street that should split a long way into two) are invisible to the heuristic.
  • Cross-zone gaps. A node disconnect whose two participating ways lie strictly in different zones (each bbox contains exactly one way of the pair) is not detected by any per-zone audit; the audit's coverage of such cross-boundary defects is consequently incomplete.
  • Cache-fallback freshness. When the Overpass API is unreachable, the pipeline falls back to a cached snapshot. Cached payloads are revalidated for non-list elements and for the <100-element sanity threshold, but a cache that is stale relative to recent OSM edits may produce results that no longer match the live OSM state.

5.2 Federal accessibility framing

49 C.F.R. § 37.121 requires that complementary paratransit service provide a level of service comparable to fixed-route service for individuals with disabilities. Whether routing failures driven by uncorrected base-map data, where they prevent navigation to residential addresses inside a designated service zone, may bear on the comparability standard under that section is a question the author recommends SORTA legal counsel review; nothing in this work should be read as a legal conclusion. The technical question of whether such failures occur is, however, what this audit is designed to answer empirically.

5.3 Generalisation

The methodology is not specific to MetroNow, to Hamilton County, or to Via Transportation. Any routing service that consumes OSM and operates inside a geography subject to the 2007–2008 TIGER bulk import is exposed to the same defect population, scaled by local contributor density. The pipeline accepts arbitrary bounding-box parameterisations and is, in principle, redeployable to any U.S. county.

6. Recommendations

The audit is presented for two audiences. To the OpenStreetMap volunteer community: every flagged way and every flagged gap carries a deep link to the iD editor and to JOSM Remote Control, pre-zoomed to the defect; the author requests evaluation and remediation of the 221 compound defects (Class AB) as the highest-priority subpopulation. To SORTA and Via Transportation: the author requests acknowledgement of the data-quality issue, engagement with Via on the contractual division of road-graph QA responsibility, and consideration of an ongoing map-quality assurance process for the four MetroNow service zones.

The complete dataset, the per-zone Excel workbooks, the interactive Leaflet dashboards, and the pipeline source are openly available at github.com/AICincy/Tiger; the dashboards are also browsable at aicincy.github.io/Tiger. Reproduction requires Python 3.11+ and the two dependencies pinned in requirements.txt; total runtime is approximately one minute per zone on a network-warm machine.
